    <span class="eyebrow">Overview</span>
    <h2>Why Buena Vista Is <em>Colorado's Best</em> Hot Springs Basecamp</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>There's a reason Buena Vista keeps showing up on every Colorado bucket list. Nestled at the foot of the Collegiate Peaks along the Arkansas River, this small Chaffee County town punches well above its weight when it comes to hot springs access. Within a roughly 20-mile radius, you'll find three distinct public soaking destinations — each with its own character, price point, and vibe — making Buena Vista one of the most efficient hot springs destinations in the entire state.</p>
      <p>The lineup spans the full spectrum. <strong>Mount Princeton Hot Springs Resort</strong> in neighboring Nathrop is a sprawling, family-friendly complex with creekside pools, a historic bathhouse, and summer waterslides. <strong>Cottonwood Hot Springs</strong>, tucked into the pines five miles west of town, is the area's original retreat — intimate, wooded, and adults-only after dark. And <strong>Charlotte Hot Springs &amp; Botanical Gardens</strong>, the newest addition to the cluster, brings three pools and a landscaped botanical garden right to the edge of downtown. You can soak at all three in a single long weekend without ever feeling rushed.</p>
      <p>This guide covers every public hot spring within a reasonable drive, honest assessments of what each one delivers, the best routes from Denver and beyond, where to base yourself overnight, and a practical day-trip itinerary for first-timers. Whether you're planning a solo retreat, a family weekend, or a ski-season detour through Chaffee County, you'll leave knowing exactly which springs to prioritize — and which ones to save for next time.</p>

    <span class="eyebrow">The Core Cluster</span>
    <h2>The Three <em>Main</em> Springs</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>These are the three springs that make Buena Vista the hot springs hub it is. All three are open to the public without requiring overnight accommodation, and all three operate year-round. Here's the honest breakdown on each.</p>

          <div class="card-prose">
            <p>Mount Princeton is the anchor of the Buena Vista hot springs cluster and, for many visitors, the reason they make the drive down from Denver in the first place. Set in Chalk Creek Canyon against the dramatic backdrop of the Chalk Cliffs — those pale, eroded bluffs that glow in afternoon light — the resort has operated in some form since 1867. What you find today is a full-service complex sprawling across 70 acres at 8,250 feet, with a range of soaking experiences that can occupy an entire day.</p>
            <p>The heart of the experience is the series of <strong>natural creekside pools</strong> along Chalk Creek — small, hand-dug pools that fill with geothermal water ranging from pleasantly warm to seriously hot. These first-come, first-served pools are the most atmospheric part of the resort; early arrivals claim them and don't leave quickly. Up the hill, the <strong>Historic Bath House</strong> dates to the resort's 19th-century origins and offers a soaking pool and exercise pool in a more sheltered setting. The crowning feature is the <strong>Infinity Pool</strong> — a large, adults-only (16+) pool perched on the hillside with sweeping views toward the Collegiate Peaks. In summer, a pair of 700-foot waterslides and a lazy river make this the liveliest option for families with kids.</p>
            <p>Be prepared for crowds on weekends, especially in summer. Weekday mornings are the move if you want to snag a creekside pool. Day passes are sold on-site with no advance reservation needed — just show up, pay at the gate, and go. Overnight lodging (rooms, creekside cabins, hillside suites) is available if you want two days of unlimited access included in your stay price.</p>

          <div class="card-prose">
            <p>Where Mount Princeton leans into scale and spectacle, Cottonwood Hot Springs leans into atmosphere. Established in 1878 as a Ute gathering place and later a homesteader retreat, this small resort sits in a dense stand of cottonwoods and aspens along Cottonwood Creek — just five miles west of downtown Buena Vista on the road to Cottonwood Pass. The setting is genuinely lovely: a wooded canyon, the sound of the creek, and pools fed by geothermal springs that have been soaking people for well over a century.</p>
            <p>There are <strong>four pools ranging from pleasantly warm to therapeutically hot</strong>, plus a cold plunge that regulars insist on using between soaks. The pools are smaller and more intimate than Mount Princeton's sprawl — this is a place for quiet conversation or contemplative silence, not splashing around. During daytime hours the space is open to all ages; after a certain hour in the evening the pools become adults-only, which draws a devoted contingent of locals who treat Cottonwood as a neighborhood institution.</p>
            <p>The on-site inn offers cabin and lodge accommodations for those who want to wake up 30 steps from the pools. <strong>Spa services including massage are available</strong> and worth booking in advance on weekends. Day passes are sold in timed entry sessions — reservations are strongly recommended, particularly on weekends between October and March when the combination of crisp air and steaming water makes this spot especially popular.</p>

          <div class="card-prose">
            <p>Charlotte Hot Springs is the newest public soaking destination in the Buena Vista area, and it's carving out a clear niche: a beautifully landscaped, downtown-adjacent experience that makes it easy to pair a soak with dinner, a coffee stop, or a browse through the shops on Main Street. The resort sits on County Road 343, just minutes from the center of town — close enough that you don't feel like you're making a commitment to a long drive.</p>
            <p>The water here is <strong>non-sulfurous and mineral-rich</strong>, drawn from the same geothermal activity that underlies all of Chaffee County. Three pools offer varying temperatures to suit different preferences. What sets Charlotte apart visually is the adjacent <strong>botanical garden</strong>, open free to the public daily regardless of whether you're soaking — a thoughtful addition that draws even non-swimmers and creates a distinctive sense of place. The overall aesthetic is polished and intentional in a way that the older springs aren't, which some visitors love and others find a touch less rugged.</p>
            <p>As the newest facility, Charlotte has the advantage of modern amenities and fresh infrastructure. It's a natural choice for those who want a central, easy, attractive hot springs experience without committing to a full resort day. <strong>Book in advance for weekends</strong> — word has spread, and weekend timed-entry slots fill up, especially in cooler months when the pools are most inviting.</p>

    <h2>Extended Radius: <em>30–60 Minutes</em> from BV</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>If you have an extra day — or if you're building a longer Chaffee County loop — these options are worth adding to the itinerary. None of them are a quick detour, but all offer something genuinely distinct from the core BV cluster.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="extended-grid">
      <div class="ext-card">
        <div class="ext-card-name">Salida Hot Springs Aquatic Center</div>
        <div class="ext-card-dist">&#126;30 miles south · ~35 min</div>
        <div class="ext-card-body">The largest indoor hot springs pool in North America — a legitimate claim. Municipally operated by the City of Salida, which means lower prices than the private resorts. The water is piped from Poncha Springs. Great for families, lap swimmers, and anyone who wants to soak without braving the elements. Private soaking tubs available by the hour.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="ext-card">
        <div class="ext-card-name">Joyful Journey Hot Springs</div>
        <div class="ext-card-dist">&#126;50 miles south · ~55 min</div>
        <div class="ext-card-body">Cross Poncha Pass into the San Luis Valley and you reach this well-regarded resort near Moffat. Multiple outdoor pools, lodging ranging from motel rooms to yurt rentals, and a more laid-back atmosphere than the Chaffee County resorts. Affordable rates make it a favorite for longer stays. Add it to a Alamosa or Great Sand Dunes trip.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="ext-card">
        <div class="ext-card-name">Valley View Hot Springs</div>
        <div class="ext-card-dist">&#126;60 miles south · ~65 min</div>
        <div class="ext-card-body">The most wild-feeling option within an hour of BV. Clothing-optional, clothing-required depends on pool area. Set on a hillside with panoramic San Luis Valley views, operated by a nonprofit. Rustic on purpose — bring your own food and towels. Reservations required; spots fill weeks ahead on popular weekends.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="ext-card">
        <div class="ext-card-name">Waunita Hot Springs Ranch</div>
        <div class="ext-card-dist">&#126;55 miles northwest · ~65 min</div>
        <div class="ext-card-body">Over Monarch Pass toward Gunnison, Waunita is primarily a private guest ranch that opens to the public when no events are scheduled. Large outdoor pool fed by natural hot springs. Call ahead before making the drive — availability varies. Beautiful high-country setting for those who want to combine hot springs with the Gunnison corridor.</div>

  <section id="routes" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Directions &amp; Access</span>
    <h2>Getting to <em>Buena Vista</em></h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>Buena Vista sits at the intersection of US-24 and US-285 in central Colorado, at an elevation of just under 8,000 feet. It's one of the more accessible mountain destinations from the Front Range — roughly 120 miles from Denver and 150 miles from Colorado Springs — and the roads in and out are generally well-maintained year-round. That said, winter driving on any of the mountain passes requires attention and sometimes traction devices, so check conditions before you leave.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="route-grid">
      <div class="route-card">
        <div class="route-label">Fastest from Denver</div>
        <div class="route-title">US-285 South through South Park — 120 mi, &#126;2 hrs</div>
        <div class="route-body">Head south on US-285 from Denver (or pick it up from C-470 / Wadsworth in the southwest suburbs). The route climbs over <strong>Kenosha Pass</strong> (10,001 ft) and drops into the sweeping South Park basin before descending to Buena Vista via Johnson Village. Straightforward two-lane highway most of the way; passes through Fairplay. Kenosha and Trout Creek passes are generally well-maintained but can be icy or snowy November through March — check CDOT conditions at cotrip.org.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="route-card route-alt">
        <div class="route-label">Alternate from Denver / I-70 Corridor</div>
        <div class="route-title">I-70 West to US-24 South through Leadville — &#126;140 mi, ~2.5 hrs</div>
        <div class="route-body">Take I-70 west to Exit 195 (Copper Mountain), then <strong>Hwy 91 south to Leadville</strong>, then US-24 south to Buena Vista. This route adds about 20–30 minutes but passes through historic Leadville (worth a stop) and offers stunning views of the Sawatch Range. A good choice for those coming from Vail, Summit County, or the I-70 mountain corridor. Tennessee Pass on this route sits at 10,424 ft — watch for conditions in winter.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="route-card route-scenic">
        <div class="route-label">Scenic Summer Route from Colorado Springs</div>
        <div class="route-title">US-24 West over Ute Pass — &#126;105 mi, ~2 hrs</div>
        <div class="route-body">From Colorado Springs, take US-24 west through Manitou Springs and Woodland Park, over Ute Pass (9,165 ft), and continue through Hartsel and into Buena Vista. <strong>Passes directly through South Park</strong> with dramatic views and access to the quirky small towns along the way. More scenic than I-25 to Pueblo and back up, and roughly the same drive time from the Springs. Note that US-24 west of Hartsel can be desolate — fuel up before you leave Woodland Park or Hartsel.</div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="honest-box" style="margin-top: 32px;">
      <div class="honest-label">Navigation Note</div>
      <p>Cell service along US-285 south of Denver and on US-24 between Hartsel and BV can be spotty. <strong>Download offline maps</strong> (Google Maps or Maps.me) before leaving a city. For Mount Princeton specifically, GPS directions to "County Road 162, Nathrop" are reliable; for Cottonwood, plug in "18999 County Road 306, Buena Vista" rather than just the name, as older GPS units sometimes route incorrectly. The road to Cottonwood along Hwy 306 is paved and well-maintained year-round.</p>
    </div>
  </section>


  
  <section id="where-to-stay" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Basing Yourself Overnight</span>
    <h2>Where to <em>Stay</em> Near Buena Vista Hot Springs</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>The obvious move is to stay on-site at one of the resorts — Mount Princeton and Cottonwood both offer overnight accommodations with hot springs access included. But downtown Buena Vista is also a legitimate basecamp, with enough lodging variety and good enough access to all three springs that it often makes more sense than committing to a single property. Here's the quick rundown by type.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="stay-grid">
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">On-Site · Resort Stay</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Mount Princeton Hot Springs Resort</div>
        <div class="stay-body">Rooms, creekside cabins, and hillside suites ranging from &#126;$200–$500/night depending on season. Overnight guests get two consecutive days of hot springs access included. Book 4–6 weeks ahead for peak summer and holiday weekends.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">On-Site · Inn &amp; Cabin Stay</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Cottonwood Hot Springs Inn &amp; Spa</div>
        <div class="stay-body">Cabins and lodge rooms; hot springs access is included with all overnight stays. More intimate and wooded than Princeton. A favorite for couples and solo travelers. Book massage appointments at the same time as your room reservation.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">Private Rental · Hot Spring Included</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Merrifield Homestead Cabins</div>
        <div class="stay-body">Two refurbished historic cabins on Cottonwood Creek, each with a private hot springs-fed soaking pool on the deck. Right next to Charlotte Hot Springs. The Merrifield family has owned this land since 1889 — a genuinely special place for those who want a private soak.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">Private Rental · Hot Spring Included</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Antero Hot Springs Cabins &amp; Treehouse</div>
        <div class="stay-body">Three private cabins in Nathrop, each with a hand-carved personal hot springs pool. The adjacent Treehouse Hot Springs offers a unique octagonal treehouse-style rental with a 27-foot private pool. Chalk Creek Canyon settings, close to Mount Princeton.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">Town Basecamp · BV Downtown</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Hotels &amp; B&amp;Bs in Buena Vista</div>
        <div class="stay-body">Staying downtown puts you 3 minutes from Charlotte, 10 minutes from Cottonwood, and 20 minutes from Mount Princeton. Options include the Surf Hotel (boutique riverfront), Buffalo Peaks Lodge (B&amp;B), and several motels. Gives you flexibility to visit multiple springs without being tethered to one property.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="stay-card">
        <div class="stay-type">Budget Option · Camping</div>
        <div class="stay-name">Dispersed &amp; Developed Camping</div>
        <div class="stay-body">San Isabel National Forest surrounding BV offers abundant dispersed camping (free, 14-day limit). Ruby Mountain Campground on the Arkansas River is a popular developed option. Camping + day passes at the springs is the most affordable multi-night strategy in the area.</div>
      </div>

  <section id="itinerary" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">How to Plan Your Visit</span>
    <h2>A <em>Full-Day</em> BV Hot Springs Itinerary</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>If you have one full day in the Buena Vista area and want to make the most of the hot springs cluster, here's a practical sequence that works well — especially on a weekday in fall or winter when crowds are lightest.</p>
      <div class="pull-quote">
        <p>Buena Vista is one of the only places in Colorado where you can soak at three genuinely different hot springs in a single day — and still have time for dinner downtown.</p>
      </div>
      <p><strong>Morning (9–11am): Charlotte Hot Springs.</strong> Start in town. Book a timed entry session for first thing in the morning, when light is best and pools are freshest. The botanical gardens are free to walk before your session. Charlotte is quick — an hour or 90 minutes is plenty — and its downtown location means you can fuel up at a BV café before or after.</p>
      <p><strong>Midday (11:30am–1:30pm): Drive to Cottonwood.</strong> It's only 10 minutes west on Hwy 306. The daytime family session at Cottonwood is calmer than evenings, and the wooded setting offers a completely different feel from Charlotte's landscaped gardens. Bring lunch — there's no food service, but the creekside setting makes a packed picnic genuinely enjoyable.</p>
      <p><strong>Afternoon (2:30–5pm): Mount Princeton.</strong> Head south on Hwy 285 to Nathrop and arrive at Mount Princeton for the afternoon session. Creekside pools are first-come, so you may need to wait for one to open up — but the bathhouse pool and hillside infinity pool are always accessible with your day pass. Plan to catch the late afternoon light on the Chalk Cliffs, which is genuinely spectacular in fall.</p>
      <p><strong>Evening: Dinner in Buena Vista.</strong> Drive the 15 miles back to BV for dinner — the town's restaurant scene has grown considerably and punches above its size. You've soaked in three distinct hot springs and logged maybe 30 miles of driving total. That's a strong day.</p>
    </div>

  <section id="faq" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Before You Go</span>
    <h2>Frequently Asked <em>Questions</em></h2>
    <div class="faq-list">

      <details>
        <summary>What hot springs are closest to Buena Vista, Colorado?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">The three closest public hot springs to Buena Vista are <strong>Charlotte Hot Springs</strong> (right in town, about 1 mile from downtown), <strong>Cottonwood Hot Springs</strong> (5 miles west on Hwy 306), and <strong>Mount Princeton Hot Springs Resort</strong> (about 15 miles south in Nathrop via Hwy 285). All three are open year-round and offer day passes without requiring overnight stays.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Can you visit multiple hot springs near Buena Vista in one day?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Yes — Charlotte, Cottonwood, and Mount Princeton are all within a 20-mile radius of downtown Buena Vista. A full day-trip hitting two or even all three is realistic, especially if you start early. Charlotte to Cottonwood is only about 10 minutes apart. Adding Mount Princeton turns it into a 30-mile loop south on Hwy 285. Budget about 90 minutes to 2 hours per spring.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Do you need reservations for hot springs near Buena Vista?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer"><strong>Mount Princeton</strong> sells day passes on-site with no reservations required for general admission — just show up. <strong>Charlotte Hot Springs</strong> and <strong>Cottonwood Hot Springs</strong> both operate on timed-entry systems, especially on weekends. Book Charlotte and Cottonwood in advance online, particularly October through March when cooler air drives demand. Private rental properties like Merrifield and Antero always require advance booking.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Are the hot springs near Buena Vista open in winter?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">All three major public springs — Charlotte, Cottonwood, and Mount Princeton — are open year-round. Winter is actually a favorite season for many regulars: the contrast between cold mountain air and steaming mineral water is hard to beat, weekday crowds thin out considerably, and the snowy Collegiate Peaks provide a dramatic backdrop. The roads to all three are paved and well-maintained. Note that Mount Princeton's waterslides and some summer amenities are not available in winter.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Are the hot springs near Buena Vista family-friendly?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer"><strong>Mount Princeton</strong> is the most family-friendly option — multiple pools at varying temperatures, a 400-foot waterslide and lazy river in summer, and a wide range of ages in the crowds. <strong>Charlotte Hot Springs</strong> welcomes all ages at all hours. <strong>Cottonwood</strong> is family-friendly during daytime hours but transitions to adults-only (18+) after dark, typically around 7 or 8pm — check their current policy when booking.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>What is the best time of year to visit hot springs near Buena Vista?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Late fall through early spring is the sweet spot for solitude-seekers — cooler air temperatures make the hot pools more enjoyable by contrast, and weekday crowds are minimal. <strong>October and November</strong> are a particular favorite: fall foliage in the Collegiate Peaks plus reliably cold mornings. Summer is peak season with the most amenities available but also the highest crowds and warmest ambient temperatures. If visiting in summer, aim for weekday mornings for the best experience.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>How far is Buena Vista from Denver for a hot springs road trip?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Buena Vista is about 120 miles southwest of Denver — roughly a 2-hour drive via <strong>Hwy 285 South</strong> through South Park, which is the fastest and most scenic route. You can also come via I-70 West to Hwy 91 South through Leadville and then Hwy 24 West, adding about 20–30 minutes but passing through Leadville (worth a stop). From Colorado Springs, US-24 West through Ute Pass is about 105 miles and roughly 2 hours.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Is there a free or primitive hot spring near Buena Vista?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">There are no well-known free primitive hot springs immediately adjacent to Buena Vista — the area's geothermal resources have largely been developed into the resorts described in this guide. The nearest low-cost option is the <strong>Salida Hot Springs Aquatic Center</strong>, about 30 miles south, which is municipally operated and charges substantially less than the private resorts (&#126;$10–15/adult). For truly primitive, free hot springs, you'd need to look further afield — Conundrum Hot Springs near Crested Butte is the nearest backcountry option, but it requires a strenuous 8.5-mile hike each way and a permit system.</div>
      </details>
