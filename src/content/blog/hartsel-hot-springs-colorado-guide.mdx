  <section id="intro" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Overview</span>
    <h2>Colorado's Best Free Soak, <em>Hidden in Plain Sight</em></h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>Most Colorado hot springs cost $20–$50 a head, require reservations, and pack in hundreds of visitors. Hartsel Hot Springs does none of those things. Tucked into the wide-open basin of South Park — about two hours from Denver and 90 minutes from Colorado Springs — these natural geothermal pools bubble up quietly along County Road 59, free to anyone willing to make the drive on a gravel road.</p>
      <p>The history here runs deep. Ute tribes used these mineral waters for centuries before European settlers arrived. Samuel Hartsel built a small resort around the springs in 1880, and the original stonework still lines sections of the pools today. What you find now is something increasingly rare in Colorado: a completely undeveloped, uncrowded, free natural hot spring with 95–110°F water, sweeping views of the Mosquito Range, and enough solitude to actually hear yourself think.</p>
      <p>This guide covers everything you need for a perfect visit — exact directions, what to pack, the best times to go, where to camp nearby, and honest answers to the questions we get asked most. Whether you're making this a day trip or pairing it with camping or hiking in the South Park area, Hartsel is worth every mile.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="getting-there" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Directions &amp; Access</span>
    <h2>Getting to <em>Hartsel Hot Springs</em></h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>Hartsel Hot Springs sits in Park County, Colorado, about 2 miles south of the small ranching town of Hartsel in the heart of the South Park basin. The elevation here hovers around 9,000 feet — noticeably thinner air if you're coming from the Front Range, so take it easy when you first arrive.</p>
      <p>From Denver (approximately 100 miles, 2 hours): Take US-285 southwest through Conifer and Kenosha Pass. At Hartsel, turn south on County Road 59 and follow it roughly 2 miles — look for the informal parking area near the river bend. From Colorado Springs (approximately 90 miles, 1 hour 45 minutes): Take US-24 west through Woodland Park and Divide, then continue west to Hartsel, where you'll turn south onto County Road 59. From Breckenridge (approximately 55 miles, 1.5 hours): Head south on CO-9 through Fairplay, then continue to Hartsel.</p>
      <p><strong>Navigation note:</strong> GPS works to Hartsel town, but cell signal fades quickly after that. Download an offline map before you leave, or note the County Road 59 turnoff from memory. The trailhead has no formal signage — slow down near the river bends and watch for parked vehicles.</p>
      <p>The last 2 miles on County Road 59 are unpaved gravel. In dry conditions, most passenger cars handle it without trouble. After rain or during winter snowmelt, the road can become muddy and deeply rutted — a high-clearance vehicle is strongly recommended in shoulder seasons, and 4WD is advisable in winter. Always check road and weather conditions before heading out, especially if you're arriving from the mountains.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="what-to-bring" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Packing List</span>
    <h2>What to <em>Bring</em></h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p><strong>The essentials — non-negotiable:</strong> swimsuit, two towels (one for the pool, one dry for the drive home), water shoes or sandals with grip, at least 2 liters of drinking water per person, sunscreen, and snacks or a full packed meal. There are zero vendors within 2 miles of the springs, and the Hartsel General Store on US-24 is your last pit stop. Bring cash — some local shops don't take cards.</p>
      <p><strong>Nice to have:</strong> a dry bag for your phone and keys, a waterproof watch (no cell signal to check the time), a lightweight camping chair or ground blanket for lounging between soaks, polarized sunglasses for the reflected glare off the water, and a light jacket or fleece — even in summer, the high-plains wind can be cold the moment you step out of the pool. If you're visiting at dawn or dusk, bring a headlamp.</p>
      <p><strong>What NOT to bring:</strong> glass bottles (breakage risk in a natural pool), large Bluetooth speakers (this is a quiet spot — other visitors will thank you), dogs if they tend to disturb wildlife, and inflatable floaties or large pool toys that will damage the natural stone edges. Leave the drone at home out of respect for wildlife and other soakers.</p>
      <div class="pull-quote">
        <p>"The moment you get out of the pool at 9,000 feet, the wind reminds you that Colorado is always colder than you expect. A dry layer waiting at the bank isn't optional — it's survival."</p>
      </div>
    </div>

  <section id="best-time" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Timing Your Visit</span>
    <h2>Best Time to <em>Visit</em> Hartsel Hot Springs</h2>
    <div class="prose">
      <p>Hartsel Hot Springs is genuinely a year-round destination, but the experience varies dramatically by season. <strong>Late spring (May–June) and early fall (September–October)</strong> hit the sweet spot: road conditions are reliable, temperatures are comfortable enough to enjoy the walk in, crowds are thin, and the surrounding grasslands are alive with wildlife and wildflowers. These months offer the best combination of accessibility and atmosphere.</p>
      <p>Summer weekends (July–August) bring the most visitors. If you're coming in peak summer, go on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning and aim to be in the water before 9am — you may have the pools entirely to yourself. <strong>Winter visits are unforgettable</strong> — steam rising from the pools against a white-dusted landscape and a sky full of stars — but require 4WD or chains, extra preparedness, and a willingness to change in sub-freezing air. Weekdays in any season are always quieter than weekends.</p>
      <h3>Quick Timing Guide</h3>
      <p>
        <strong>Best overall:</strong> September–October — crisp air, golden light, thin crowds, stable roads<br />
        <strong>Best for solitude:</strong> Weekday mornings, any season; winter visits (with proper gear)<br />
        <strong>Best light for photos:</strong> Sunrise (dawn steam and golden hour) or 30 minutes before sunset<br />
        <strong>Avoid:</strong> Summer holiday weekends (Memorial Day, 4th of July, Labor Day) and immediately after heavy rain or snow (muddy road)
      </p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="faq" class="section-block">
    <span class="eyebrow">Before You Go</span>
    <h2>Frequently Asked <em>Questions</em></h2>
    <div class="faq-list">

      <details>
        <summary>Is Hartsel Hot Springs free to visit?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Yes — Hartsel Hot Springs is completely free. There's no entry fee, no ticket booth, and no reservation required. You simply park (also free) and walk to the pools. Keep in mind there are also zero amenities, so come fully self-sufficient.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>What is the water temperature at Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">The pools typically range from <strong>95°F to 110°F</strong>. There are two main pools — one runs cooler (around 95–100°F) and is more approachable for longer soaks or visitors sensitive to heat. The second pool is hotter (105–110°F) and intense, especially at altitude. Temperatures can vary slightly by season as flow rates change.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>How do I get to Hartsel Hot Springs from Denver?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">From Denver, take US-285 southwest approximately 100 miles (about 2 hours) to the town of Hartsel. Turn south on County Road 59 and follow the gravel road for about 2 miles — watch for an informal parking area near a river bend. <strong>Download your maps offline</strong> before leaving the Front Range, as cell signal disappears around Fairplay. There is no formal signage for the springs themselves.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Are there bathrooms or changing rooms at Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">No — Hartsel Hot Springs has <strong>no restrooms, changing rooms, showers, or any built facilities</strong>. Plan to arrive in your swimwear, or change discreetly at your vehicle. The nearest public restroom is at the Hartsel General Store on US-24, about 2 miles away.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>What should I bring to Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Essentials: swimsuit, two towels, water shoes with grip, plenty of drinking water (2+ liters per person), sunscreen, snacks or a full meal, cash, and layers to change into after soaking. Helpful extras: dry bag for phone/keys, headlamp for dawn or dusk visits, and an offline map. <strong>Do not bring glass bottles or large speakers.</strong> Pack out all trash — there are no bins on-site.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>What is the best time to visit Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer"><strong>Late spring (May–June) and early fall (September–October)</strong> are the best overall windows — mild weather, minimal crowds, and reliable road conditions. For solitude, visit on a weekday morning in any season. Winter soaking is uniquely beautiful but requires a 4WD vehicle and extra gear. Avoid summer holiday weekends (Memorial Day, 4th of July, Labor Day) when parking can overflow.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Is Hartsel Hot Springs suitable for kids?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Kids can enjoy Hartsel with proper supervision. The pools are shallow (2–3 feet) but have <strong>slippery rock surfaces and hot water up to 110°F</strong>, which may be too intense for young children. The cooler pool is better suited for families. There are no lifeguards or safety equipment on-site — parental supervision is essential at all times.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Can I camp near Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Yes — several great options are within 30 minutes. <strong>Fourmile Campground</strong> in Pike National Forest is about 25 minutes south with reservable tent and RV sites. <strong>Antero Reservoir SWA</strong> offers free dispersed camping about 15 minutes northwest. <strong>Buffalo Peaks Wilderness</strong> allows backcountry camping for hikers. Fairplay (20 minutes north) has motels, cabins, and RV parks.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>Is there cell service at Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">Essentially none. <strong>Cell service becomes unreliable around Fairplay and disappears near the springs.</strong> Download Google Maps or Gaia GPS offline for the area before leaving home. Tell someone your itinerary and expected return time. Keep your gas tank full — the nearest reliable gas station is in Hartsel or Fairplay.</div>
      </details>

      <details>
        <summary>What is the road like to Hartsel Hot Springs?<span class="faq-icon">+</span></summary>
        <div class="faq-answer">US-285 and US-24 to Hartsel are fully paved. <strong>County Road 59, the last 2 miles to the springs, is unpaved gravel.</strong> In dry summer conditions, most passenger cars handle it fine. After rain, during snowmelt, or in winter, the road can become muddy and rutted — high-clearance or 4WD is recommended. Always check current conditions before heading out, especially in spring and fall.</div>
      </details>

    </div>
